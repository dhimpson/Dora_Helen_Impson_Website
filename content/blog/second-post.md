# Historical Silences and the Struggle to Remember

A few years ago, my kids and I visited Monticello. After touring the main floor of the house and marveling at the many curiosities that it contained, we made our way to the basement, where enslaved persons toiled to make the beautiful environment above the stairs possible. We sat in the room that historians believed to belong to Sally Hemmings and watched the digital presentation of her life. We toured the reconstructed cabins that housed many of those enslaved on the plantation. All of this was moving and meaningful, but there was one moment on the "slave tour" that stopped me in my tracks. The tour guide mentioned that historians working on the plantation were struggling to identify and tell the stories of field slaves. These enslaved persons were said to live on the edges of Jefferson's fields in temporary housing and were required to shift that housing according to what field demanded their attention. 

The struggle for modern-day historians and archeologists was that there was not much left behind regarding these people. The idea that I cannot tell someone's story because there is nothing left of them to tell, aside from purchase orders in a ledger and perhaps a notation of their death, profoundly struck me. The fabric of these people's lives mattered; their stories speak to the injustice of slavery that fueled our country. And yet, they exist as ghosts in the narrative of American history. That day sparked an interest in uncovering historical silences in America's history and doing my best to assist in helping a nation that is far too comfortable forgetting, to remember.

In the past few years, Clemson University has been on a journey to uncover and remember enslaved persons who lived on the plantation where the University was established. If you have been to a football game at Death Valley, you have probably noticed that the stadium shares a hill with a cemetery. On this hill lies the family graves of those who originally owned the plantation, as well as many former University Presidents and faculty, as well as hundreds of unmarked graves of formerly enslaved people, convicts who were leased to build the University, and many others. The stories of these people have largely gone untold until recently. Advancements in ground penetrating technology have enabled the University to identify these graves and commemorate them with a granite marker. Last March, I toured the graveyard after the grave markers were placed and was moved by the overwhelming effect of so many markers. Each one is an effort to recall, remember, and honor someone who lived, breathed, and died. They are simple markers, painfully simple, but powerful in stating that these people mattered.
