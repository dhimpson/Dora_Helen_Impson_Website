Facts, Sources, and Fallacies

STATING FACTS AND CITING SOURCES

This week’s assignment is two parts: First I will be looking at a historical assertion from one of my sources and then I will examine that assertion in two other historical works, looking closely at how other historians interpret that assertion.

I looked at the assertion Richard Rothstein makes in his book The Color of Law: A Forgotten History of How Our Government Segregated America, in which Rothstein argues that segregation in the United States was intentional, occurred by government action and therefore is de facto and not de jure. Rothstein says that such segregation is by law and public policy and not by personal decision or choice. 1 To check this claim, I searched through James Kushner’s foundational work Apartheid in America: An Historical and Legal Analysis of Contemporary Racial Residential Segregation in the United States, which reinforces the claim that America’s segregation was de facto instead of de jure. Rothstein based much of his argument on Kushner’s legal journal article. Cross-checking Rothstein’s citation also allowed me to see Kushner’s argument in its original context, which also helped me to gain a better grasp of de facto and de jure segregation.

The second part of this assignment concerned reading the introduction of David Hackett Fischer’s Historians’ Fallacies: Toward a Logic of Historical Thought and familiarizing myself with Fischer’s three categories of fallacies and their respective chapters. I then chose one of Fischer’s fallacies to apply to a historical source from my research.

Fischer defines a historical fallacy as errors in historical scholarship that lead to false assumptions and procedures. He specifically notes that “a fallacy is not merely an error in and of itself, but a way of falling into error."1 and breaks such fallacies into three categories: fallacies of Inquiry, fallacies of explanation, and fallacies of argument. For my assignment, I chose to look at James Lowen’s 2009 book Sundown Towns, which makes the sweeping claim that the southern United States escaped Sundown Towns that plagued the Northern and Western portions of the U.S., through the lens of Fischer’s fallacy called the overwhelming exception. This fallacy makes sweeping generalizations that lead the reader to falsely believe something to be true. For example, Loewen claims that Sundown towns were not common in the South. By making this argument, he fails to note that Southern states were not only home to Sundown towns and counties, Forsyth County in Georgia is a notable example, but he also leads the reader to create a false assumption that perhaps racial issues in Southern towns were not as bad as their Northern counterparts.
