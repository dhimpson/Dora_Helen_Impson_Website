Time in the Archive

This week, I visited the Clemson University Special Collections and Archives to get hands-on archival experience. Before I visited the archive, my class was given a list of primary source materials to choose a topic. I chose The James F. Byrnes papers. I knew a little about Byrnes, as he had popped up in my research, but I needed to know more about him. I did a quick Google search to help me choose what possible topics from Byrnes’s life might be interesting to me. I used the finding aides on the Special Collections website to assist me in narrowing down my selections from the 3,000 plus items that are included in Byrne’s papers. I decided to focus on Byrnes’s sixteen-month stent as a justice of the United States Supreme Court, which occurred during World War II. This time period is confined to sources from Series 3 of the collection. I then requested Boxes 1, 2, and 6. These boxes contained various folders with mixed materials.

I also found that I could use the finding aids to get a better understanding of what materials are inside of this box. The screenshot below displays the box’s folders and a description of what each folder might contain.

I opened box six first and started going through each folder, one at a time, while taking notes on anything that caught my attention. Byrnes had spent many years as a U.S. Senator for South Carolina before his appointment to the U.S. Supreme Court. This box contained correspondence from his former constituents who still wanted his help in matters back in South Carolina. Each letter that was written to Byrnes had an attached copy of his response, stating that he could not offer assistance due to his position on the Supreme Court. A few of these letters caught my attention.

In box 6, folder 1, which is labeled as Professional Correspondence, I found this letter from the Royal Cola Company, also known as RC Cola, asking for assistance in obtaining more sugar to make their colas. The cola company noted that due to sugar rations imposed by the government to help with the war efforts, they could not meet the demands that had been placed on them, specifically noting that they supply cola to soldiers. A letter such as this, could be helpful for historical researchers who research the economic impact of war upon local private companies. How did rationing create issues with supply and demand in local economies?

As I worked, I took pictures of things that interested me. I uploaded them to Tropy and entered metadata for each item so that I could construct a bibliography to use later. Below is a screenshot showing the letter from RC Cola in Tropy, along with its metadata.

As I mentioned earlier, South Carolinians continued to write to Byrnes for assistance after he was appointed to the Supreme Court. Many of these letters survived and were tucked into several of the folders that I came across in my research. These letters often were from parents of soldiers who were writing to Byrnes on behalf of their “boys,” asking for better provisions in the field. Although I grew up surrounded by World War II veterans, I was not aware of the shortages that crippled the armed forces until I read these letters. Byrnes eventually stepped down from the Supreme Court to assist the Roosevelt administration in the war effort, specifically working on how to ensure supply and demand were accomplished. These letters would make an interesting research project into how the public moved government officials to become better stewards of materials in order to further the war effort.

A letter asking Justice Byrnes to help “our Hartsville boys”

Byrnes and President Franklin D. Roosevelt shared a friendship that was demonstrated in their correspondence. The Telegraph below shows Byrnes wishing the President Happy Birthday, and the memo contains a joke about both of them being rebuffed by the State Department. Items such as these personal notes help to bring a fullness to historical actors and also can explain why someone like Byrnes might step down from a life appointment to the Supreme Court to work in the Roosevelt administration. Not only had Byrnes had a first-hand glimpse of wartime shortages and their effects on soldiers from the many letters that he had received from his parents, but also his welcoming of an administration job allowed him to work closely with Roosevelt as a trusted friend and ally.

This was a fun exercise and provided me with some valuable time understanding how an archive works.
